# Death Scenario

## The Death (Summary)
**Method:** Chloramine gas poisoning from mixing bleach (sodium hypochlorite) with ammonia-based cleaner in enclosed bathroom
**Category:** Environmental Hazards
**Duration:** Minutes (approximately 8-12 minutes from first exposure to death)
**Witnesses:** None during death; other guests hear his distress but cannot enter locked bathroom

## Mechanism (Technical)

### The Cause
Chloramine gas (NH2Cl) toxicity. When bleach and ammonia combine, they produce chloramine vapors that cause severe respiratory damage. In an enclosed space with poor ventilation, concentrations quickly become lethal.

### The Setup
- Small hostel bathroom with no windows
- Poor ventilation (exhaust fan broken for months)
- Teddy brings industrial-strength bleach and multiple cleaning products
- Moroccan hostel also uses ammonia-based floor cleaner (residue on tiles)
- Teddy locks door to prevent interruption during cleaning ritual

### The Trigger
Teddy pours concentrated bleach onto bathroom floor still wet with ammonia-based cleaner. The chemical reaction begins immediately, producing visible vapor.

### The Process

1. Teddy enters bathroom at 2:34 PM with cleaning supplies
2. Locks door (essential for uninterrupted ritual)
3. Notices ammonia smell from recent staff cleaning
4. Rather than waiting, pours bleach to "neutralize" perceived contamination
5. Chemical reaction begins—yellowish-green vapor rises
6. First symptoms: eye burning, throat irritation (dismissed as "strong cleaner smell")
7. Continues cleaning despite discomfort (ritual must be completed)
8. Coughing begins, increasingly violent
9. Attempts to open door—coordination failing, drops key
10. Pounds on door, too weak to be heard clearly
11. Collapses near toilet, still trying to reach door
12. Pulmonary edema fills lungs with fluid
13. Death by asphyxiation within 12 minutes of initial exposure

## Research Notes
- Chloramine gas: Toxic at concentrations above 1 ppm; produces at higher rates in enclosed spaces
- Symptoms: Eye/nose/throat irritation, coughing, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, pulmonary edema
- Lethal exposure: 10+ minutes in concentrated environment can cause fatal pulmonary damage
- Common accident: CDC reports hundreds of injuries annually from accidental chemical mixing
- Source: CDC chemical safety reports, poison control center data, case studies of accidental chloramine exposure

## The Death Sequence (Full Detail)

### Setup (Chapter 35-37)
Teddy has been at the hostel for two days. His anxiety has been building—the bathroom never feels clean enough. He overhears staff cleaning and smells ammonia. He plans a "deep clean" during siesta when the bathroom will be empty. He gathers his supplies. He tells no one.

### Trigger (Chapter 38)
At 2:34 PM, Teddy locks the bathroom door. The floor is still damp from staff cleaning. He can smell ammonia. Rather than see this as a sign the bathroom is already clean, he sees it as contamination requiring neutralization. He opens his industrial bleach.

### The Death (Chapters 39-40)

**Minute 0:**
The bleach hits the tile. Immediately, a sharp, acrid smell—different from normal bleach. A yellowish haze begins to rise. Teddy's eyes water. He blinks, continues pouring. The ritual must be completed.

**Minute 1-2:**
Throat burning. He coughs once, twice. The air feels thick. He tells himself: strong cleaners smell strong. He starts scrubbing the toilet, breathing through his mouth. The taste is like swimming pool chemicals times a hundred.

**Minute 3-5:**
The coughing won't stop. Each breath is a rasp. He stands, dizzy, and the bathroom tilts. The vapor is visible now—greenish, hanging at knee level, rising. He realizes something is wrong. He moves toward the door.

**Minute 6-8:**
His hands are shaking. The lock—he can't—the key slips from his latex-gloved fingers, falls into the vapor cloud. He drops to his knees to find it, putting his face directly into the concentrated gas. His lungs feel like they're filling with acid. He screams but it comes out as a wheeze.

**Minute 9-12:**
He pounds on the door with weakening fists. Someone outside asks "Everything okay?" He can't form words. His vision is going grey. He thinks about his sister, about his mother, about how he just wanted things to be clean. His lungs are drowning in fluid. The tile is cold against his cheek—at least it's clean now.

**Final Moment:**
His last breath is a gurgle. His body convulses once. His hand is still reaching toward the door.

### Immediate Aftermath
Guests hear the final pounding, call for staff. Manager breaks down door after 20 minutes of no response. Vapor cloud escapes, causing several people to cough. Teddy is found face-down, skin flushed pink, foam at his mouth. His latex gloves are still on.

### Sound

- His own coughing, wet and desperate

### Touch/Pain

- Skin: Tingling, then burning where vapor touches
- Hands: Shaking uncontrollably

### Smell
- Initial: Sharp chlorine/bleach
- Then: Acrid, choking, indescribable
- Finally: Nothing (olfactory system overwhelmed)

### Taste
- Pool chemicals, concentrated a thousandfold
- Copper (blood from damaged airways)
- The taste of his own mortality

## The Horror Element
He did this to himself. His need for control created the very chaos that kills him. He's locked in a tiny room with poison he created, and the thing that was supposed to protect him (the locked door) is what prevents his escape. His last moments are spent reaching for a door he chose to lock.

## The Comedy Element
The dark irony is total: he feared contamination, and he contaminated himself to death. His Instagram followers will later debate whether his death was "authentic" or staged. The bathroom, by the way, is spotless. Best it's ever looked.

- Slow build (two days of escalating anxiety)
- Fast death (12 minutes from trigger to end)

### POV During Death
- Stay in Teddy's head throughout
- His internal monologue shifts from ritual-focused to panic to acceptance
- The OCD voice keeps trying to rationalize even as he dies

### Graphic Content Level
- 8/10: Explicit respiratory distress, chemical burns, the physical horror of drowning in your own lungs
- Show the panic, the failed escape attempt, the foam
- The clinical detail serves the theme: he understood contamination, and contamination killed him
